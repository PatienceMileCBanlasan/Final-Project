Deborah's story, shared by the Victorian Equal Opportunity and Human Rights Commission, illustrates a case of workplace sex discrimination. After being hired as a bus driver—a role for which she was well-qualified due to her experience driving heavy vehicles in the Royal Australian Air Force—Deborah faced discriminatory treatment from her employer. Despite successfully completing her training, her manager expressed doubts about her ability to handle the job solely because she was a woman, insisting she remain on a lower training wage until she "proved herself." This stance was based on the belief that bus driving was "man's work," without any performance-related justification. Feeling that this was unjust, Deborah reported the incident to the company's HR department and filed a formal complaint with the Commission. As a result, the company apologized, committed to providing equal opportunity training, and ensured she received the appropriate wage. This case underscores that, under Victoria's Equal Opportunity Act, sex discrimination is unlawful at all stages of employment, including recruitment, training, and remuneration. Employers are obligated to treat all employees fairly, regardless of gender. If you believe you've experienced discrimination, sexual harassment, victimization, or vilification in Victoria, you can seek assistance or file a complaint through the Victorian Equal Opportunity and Human Rights Commission.

In “My Girl Child Long Walk Story: A Journey in Progress Towards Gender Equality,” Obi Chuks shares a deeply personal reflection on his evolving understanding of gender inequality and his commitment to challenging the systems that sustain it. He begins by acknowledging how society, institutions, and religion have historically framed women as subordinate to men. For a long time, he accepted these roles as natural, never questioning the way power was distributed based on gender. However, through reflection and learning, Obi began to realize that many of the assumptions about gender roles are not inherent but socially constructed. He points out that differences between men and women—biological or otherwise—should not justify unequal treatment or determine one’s opportunities in life. He criticizes the way patriarchy has been normalized, shaping attitudes and practices that limit the rights, choices, and futures of girls and women. Obi's transformation is ongoing, but he now firmly believes that society must move toward recognizing and nurturing the potential of every individual, regardless of gender. He calls for a cultural shift in which competence, not gender, defines a person’s worth or opportunity. His story is not just about personal growth—it’s a call to action for others to reflect, challenge inherited norms, and contribute to a future where no girl is denied her rights, education, or dreams simply because of her gender. His narrative is part of the larger “Girl Child Long Walk” movement, which seeks to amplify stories and voices advocating for gender equality, especially in communities where girls continue to face systemic barriers to full participation in society.
